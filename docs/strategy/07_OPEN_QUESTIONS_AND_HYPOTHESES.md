# Open Questions and Hypotheses

## Purpose

This document captures what still needs to be validated. The goal is to prevent false certainty and keep the strategy honest while you iterate.

## Market questions

### 1) Which ICP is strongest first?
Possible answers:
- breakaway advisors pre-launch,
- newly registered RIAs,
- existing small RIAs with messy compliance,
- outsourced CCOs / consultants.

**Hypothesis:** breakaway + newly launched RIAs will be the most emotionally urgent wedge, while consultants may become the best multiplier channel.

### 2) Is the first buyer the founder or the compliance lead?
**Hypothesis:** the founder buys the launch story; the CCO or ops lead becomes the power user after launch.

### 3) Is state-registered or SEC-registered the better starting lane?
**Hypothesis:** the product should support both conceptually, but early sales might be easier where pain, complexity, and willingness to pay are highest.

### 4) How much of the market actually wants software versus service?
**Hypothesis:** many firms want software plus confidence, which means a software-only motion may undershoot what the customer actually wants early on.

## Product questions

### 5) Is launch really the best wedge, or is ongoing exam readiness the better entry?
**Hypothesis:** launch is the sharper narrative and the stronger emotional wedge, but ongoing compliance may be easier to monetize repeatedly.

### 6) Which module creates the fastest “aha” moment?
Candidates:
- launch wizard,
- obligations engine,
- dashboard,
- annual review,
- exam room,
- marketing review.

**Hypothesis:** the first “aha” likely comes from the launch workspace plus a dynamic dashboard that clearly shows what matters now.

### 7) How far should V1 go into operations?
Potential adjacencies:
- CRM overlays,
- client communication,
- performance snapshots,
- billing oversight,
- vendor orchestration.

**Hypothesis:** expansion into operations should happen only after compliance becomes the trusted backbone.

### 8) Should the product include a consultant workspace in V1?
**Hypothesis:** not as a headline feature, but the data model should allow it because consultants could become an important channel.

### 9) Is marketing review the right first deep module after core compliance?
**Hypothesis:** yes, because it is concrete, current, recurring, and understandable to buyers.

### 10) Should private fund workflows be in scope early?
**Hypothesis:** no for V1. Keep the architecture extensible, but do not let private fund complexity distort the initial product.

## AI questions

### 11) Will users trust AI in this context?
**Hypothesis:** they will trust it only if the product makes AI outputs:
- transparent,
- source-backed,
- editable,
- reviewable,
- clearly non-final until approved.

### 12) Where does AI add the most value first?
Potential areas:
- explaining tasks,
- summarizing rules,
- generating draft policies/checklists,
- spotting missing information,
- summarizing annual review gaps,
- assisting with marketing review.

**Hypothesis:** explanation and draft-generation are the least controversial and most useful starting points.

### 13) What AI use cases are too risky early?
**Hypothesis:** autonomous decisions, direct filing automation, and black-box “compliant / non-compliant” judgments are too risky for the first versions.

## Commercial questions

### 14) What pricing structure will feel natural?
Options:
- one-time launch fee + recurring subscription,
- purely recurring subscription,
- modular subscription,
- software + managed support.

**Hypothesis:** launch fee + recurring subscription is the cleanest initial posture.

### 15) Is this a direct-sales business or a partner-led business?
**Hypothesis:** direct founder-led sales first, then partner-led expansion through consultants and related ecosystems.

### 16) Can consultants be collaborators instead of competitors?
**Hypothesis:** yes. The product can become the operating layer experts use with clients, which could be a stronger path than trying to replace them immediately.

### 17) How much of the budget is rationalized as risk reduction versus labor savings?
**Hypothesis:** the strongest buyer story combines both, but emotional relief and confidence may be stronger than pure ROI language in early sales.

## Compliance / legal boundary questions

### 18) Where is the product boundary between workflow assistance and legal advice?
**Hypothesis:** the product should operationalize process, documentation, sequencing, and guidance, while preserving human review for decisions and firm-specific legal judgment.

### 19) How opinionated should the product be?
**Hypothesis:** very opinionated in workflow design, less opinionated in areas that require firm-specific legal tailoring.

### 20) How much template content should exist in-product?
**Hypothesis:** enough to accelerate work, but not so much that the product drifts into pretending every firm can be standardized identically.

## Distribution questions

### 21) Which channels are most credible?
Candidates:
- compliance consultants,
- law firms,
- custody transition support,
- advisor communities,
- direct content/SEO,
- referrals from existing users.

**Hypothesis:** a combination of founder-led direct sales + content + consultant relationships is likely strongest early.

### 22) What lead magnets are most compelling?
Candidates:
- launch readiness assessment,
- registration path checker,
- annual review checklist,
- exam readiness score,
- Reg S-P small-firm checklist.

**Hypothesis:** a launch readiness tool and exam readiness tool are probably the strongest.

## Hypotheses to test in the first 20–30 interviews

1. Advisors are more willing to buy “clarity and control” than “AI.”
2. The launch moment is painful enough to drive urgency.
3. Buyers want workflow plus evidence, not just checklists.
4. Consultants will use software if it makes them more scalable.
5. Customers still want human review even when they like AI assistance.
6. The strongest long-term value is in becoming the firm’s operating memory.
7. Marketing review and annual review are better module expansions than broad CRM features.
8. The product earns trust fastest when it explains why each task exists.

## Kill criteria / warning signs

You should rethink the concept if several of these prove true:

- buyers mostly want a service and do not care about software;
- incumbents already solve the workflow pain well enough for the target segment;
- launch pain is not monetizable;
- firms do not want to keep living in the product after launch;
- the product naturally drifts into a generic task manager;
- AI does not create enough differentiated value even when used carefully.

## Most important unresolved decision

The single most important unresolved question is:

**Are you building a software-first company with expert assist, or a services-heavy company with enabling software?**

That answer will affect:
- product depth,
- pricing,
- sales motion,
- hiring,
- margins,
- and how investors/customers understand the company.

My current working bias is:
**software-first, with strategic service and expert-review layers early to accelerate trust.**
