# Working Business Plan — RIA Launch & Compliance OS

## Status

This is a working business plan, not a polished investor memo. It is meant to clarify strategy, priorities, and business logic so you can iterate with Codex and with real customer conversations.

## 1) Company thesis

We are building an **RIA Launch & Compliance OS** for small and emerging advisory firms.

The product starts by solving the hardest, most intimidating part of independence:

- becoming an RIA,
- understanding what needs to be filed and when,
- building the required policies, workflows, and controls,
- and staying examination-ready after launch.

Over time, the product expands into selected operating workflows that sit naturally adjacent to compliance and governance.

## 2) Problem

For many prospective and newly independent advisors, the move to independence is blocked or slowed by a dense mix of regulatory, operational, and project-management friction.

The current experience is often something like this:

- unclear decision tree;
- fragmented professional advice;
- templates without context;
- too many deadlines to manage manually;
- weak visibility into what is complete versus still exposed;
- too much dependence on a single CCO, consultant, or founder;
- little confidence that the firm would perform well in an exam.

The firm does not just need information. It needs an operating system.

## 3) Customer segments

### Primary ICP: breakaway and newly launched RIAs
Typical profile:

- solo founder or small founding team;
- coming from a wirehouse, broker-dealer, or established advisory platform;
- highly competent as an advisor, but not excited about regulatory project management;
- wants independence without drowning in back-office complexity.

### Secondary ICP: existing small RIAs with underbuilt compliance infrastructure
Typical profile:

- already live;
- small team;
- compliance process lives across memory, spreadsheets, email, and point tools;
- wants more structure before the next exam or operational growth phase.

### Tertiary ICP: outsourced CCOs and compliance consultants
Typical profile:

- supports multiple firms;
- repetitive manual work;
- wants workflow standardization, evidence capture, and a scalable client operating layer.

## 4) Jobs to be done

The product should win because it helps a customer do the following jobs:

### Functional jobs
- Tell me what I need to do to become an RIA.
- Turn rules and filings into an understandable sequence.
- Keep me on top of deadlines and recurring obligations.
- Help me gather and store the right evidence.
- Make annual reviews and exam prep less painful.
- Reduce dependence on scattered manual processes.

### Emotional jobs
- Reduce intimidation.
- Reduce fear of missing something material.
- Create confidence that the firm is “buttoned up.”
- Make independence feel manageable, not reckless.

### Social / reputational jobs
- Help a founder look competent and organized.
- Help a CCO demonstrate control.
- Help the firm show regulators, consultants, and partners that it takes governance seriously.

## 5) Product summary

### Core promise
**From launch confusion to operational compliance clarity.**

### V1
A multi-tenant web application that includes:

- launch/readiness questionnaire,
- registration and setup workflow,
- compliance calendar,
- obligation engine,
- policy/document center,
- evidence capture,
- annual review workflow,
- exam room,
- marketing review workflow,
- Reg S-P / vendor oversight readiness scaffolding,
- AI-guided but human-reviewed recommendations.

### V2+
Expand into:
- integrations with CRM and selected advisor stack systems,
- client communication governance,
- broader operational dashboards,
- optional private fund modules,
- consultant / outsourced-CCO workspace.

## 6) Why customers will pay

The product saves money in three ways:

1. fewer manual hours;
2. fewer consultant/law-firm hours for repetitive operational work;
3. lower risk of deadline misses, weak documentation, and compliance disorder.

It also creates value in three ways:

1. faster path to independence,
2. better confidence and control for founders,
3. cleaner readiness for exams and diligence.

The strongest ROI argument is probably not “this is cheaper than everything.” It is:

**this replaces a chaotic, high-friction process with a structured system that reduces risk and saves senior attention.**

## 7) Business model hypotheses

These are hypotheses to test, not claims about final pricing.

### Option A — Setup fee + recurring SaaS
Best if launch is the wedge.

- one-time launch/onboarding package;
- recurring monthly or annual subscription for ongoing compliance use.

This likely maps well to customer psychology because “launch” feels like a project and “compliance” feels like a system.

### Option B — Subscription tiers by complexity
Charge based on firm complexity rather than just seats.

Possible drivers:
- SEC vs state registration,
- number of supervised persons,
- number of offices,
- private fund complexity,
- optional modules (marketing review, vendor oversight, exam room, consultant collaboration).

### Option C — Software + managed support
A hybrid model where software is the core, but onboarding and premium support are monetized.

This may be the best early model because trust in this category is high-value and expert-heavy.

## 8) Pricing hypotheses

Illustrative, not final:

### Starter
For solo / very small firms:
- lower monthly fee,
- core launch workflow,
- compliance calendar,
- document center,
- basic annual review / exam room.

### Growth
For small multi-person RIAs:
- higher monthly fee,
- added marketing review,
- more robust vendor and supervisory workflows,
- consultant collaboration,
- role-based permissions.

### Premium / managed
For firms wanting more support:
- white-glove onboarding,
- expert review packages,
- outsourced/partner compliance support layer,
- implementation help.

A sensible principle is that the product should feel materially cheaper than staffing more manual compliance overhead, but expensive enough to signal seriousness.

## 9) Go-to-market hypotheses

### Founder-led sales first
Early customers should probably be won through direct conversations and highly consultative demos.

Why:
- the product category is trust-heavy,
- messaging will need refinement,
- you will learn more from early close/loss patterns than from broad top-of-funnel activity.

### Content-led demand
Strong content themes:
- how to start an RIA,
- state vs SEC registration basics,
- what an annual review actually needs,
- exam readiness checklists,
- marketing rule review mistakes,
- Regulation S-P implementation for small firms,
- AI governance for advisory firms.

This category lends itself to useful, credible content.

### Channel / partner GTM
Potential channels:
- compliance consultants,
- outsourced CCOs,
- law firms serving emerging RIAs,
- custodial transition consultants,
- advisor communities and independence coaches.

The best early partner strategy may be “make experts faster,” not “disintermediate experts.”

## 10) Why this could become defensible

### 1. Workflow data and firm memory
Once a firm lives in the product, the system becomes its operating memory:
- deadlines,
- policies,
- approvals,
- artifacts,
- reviews,
- audit trail,
- exam readiness history.

That is sticky.

### 2. Regulatory ontology
A real moat can emerge if you build a structured obligations graph:
- what obligations exist,
- which firm characteristics trigger them,
- what evidence satisfies them,
- what cadence applies,
- who owns them,
- what regulators typically examine around them.

That is more defensible than a thin UI layer.

### 3. AI grounded in workflow, not generic chat
A generic LLM is easy to replicate. An AI layer grounded in:
- firm profile,
- actual tasks,
- actual documents,
- approvals,
- cited source material,
- and audit trail

is harder to replace.

### 4. Embedded distribution through consultants and ecosystems
If consultants and adjacent platforms start using the product as the operating layer, switching costs and data gravity improve.

## 11) Key strategic risks

### Risk 1 — Product sprawl
The obvious adjacency set is huge. The company can easily lose focus.

**Mitigation:** hold the line on launch + compliance wedge.

### Risk 2 — Legal/compliance liability
If the product sounds like legal advice, risk goes up quickly.

**Mitigation:** keep a clear product boundary, human approvals, source-backed guidance, and explicit disclaimers.

### Risk 3 — Trust barrier around AI
Some firms may resist AI in compliance contexts.

**Mitigation:** use AI as a draft/recommendation/flagging layer with explainability and approvals.

### Risk 4 — Long sales cycles
Advisors and compliance buyers may buy slowly.

**Mitigation:** target high-intent moments like launch, transition, regulatory change, annual review, or exam prep.

### Risk 5 — Existing incumbents add features
Incumbents can add modules.

**Mitigation:** own the integrated narrative and workflow from day zero, not just one more feature.

## 12) Initial moat-friendly positioning

The product should be positioned less like “software to manage compliance tasks” and more like:

**the system that operationalizes the entire path from independence decision to ongoing examination readiness.**

That is a much stronger frame.

## 13) Strategic product boundaries

### Definitely in scope early
- launch readiness
- registration workflow
- task orchestration
- filing calendar / cadence
- policy and template center
- evidence capture
- annual review
- exam room
- marketing review
- vendor and incident-readiness scaffolding
- role-based workflow

### Explicitly out of scope early
- direct live filing into IARD/EDGAR in V1
- custody infrastructure
- performance reporting engine
- portfolio accounting
- full CRM replacement
- generalized portfolio ops platform
- legal opinion engine

## 14) 12-month operating plan (working draft)

### Months 0–2
- 20–30 customer discovery calls
- design-partner outreach
- Codex prototype
- landing page + narrative
- clickable workflow demo

### Months 2–4
- concierge MVP with a few real or simulated firms
- validate onboarding flow
- validate obligations engine logic
- validate exam room / evidence workflow

### Months 4–6
- pilot with 3–10 design partners
- refine role model, calendar logic, and recurring obligations
- tighten marketing review and annual review workflows

### Months 6–9
- first paid pilots
- add consultant-facing collaboration layer
- improve audit trail and report exports
- package clear pricing

### Months 9–12
- narrow on the most resonant ICP
- add selected integrations
- begin systematizing GTM with content + partners
- decide whether to deepen compliance or broaden into ops

## 15) Potential company narrative

A strong plain-English narrative:

> Independent advisors should not need a patchwork of consultants, templates, calendars, and tribal knowledge to become and run an RIA. We turn that complexity into a single, guided, exam-ready operating system.

## 16) What success looks like

The clearest signs this is working:

- breakaway advisors say the product made independence feel possible;
- newly launched RIAs keep using it after registration;
- CCOs use it weekly, not just quarterly;
- firms store evidence in the product by default;
- consultants want to manage clients inside it;
- customers describe it as “the place I go to know what matters now.”

## Final recommendation

The business is worth pursuing if you commit to three things:

1. stay narrow on the initial wedge;
2. use software to productize expert process, not just digitize checklists;
3. keep trust and auditability at the center of the product design.

If you do that, this can be more than a compliance tool. It can become the backbone layer for modern small RIAs.
